Introduction

Chemical kinetics is the branch of chemistry that studies the rate of chemical reactions and the factors that affect them.

While thermodynamics tells whether a reaction is possible, chemical kinetics explains how fast the reaction occurs.

Understanding reaction rates is important in industrial chemistry, environmental chemistry, and biological systems.

Key Definitions

  • Rate of Reaction: Change in concentration of reactants or products per unit time.
  • Average Rate: Rate measured over a specific time interval.
  • Instantaneous Rate: Rate at a particular moment of time.
  • Rate Law: Mathematical relationship between reaction rate and concentration.
  • Order of Reaction: Sum of powers of concentration terms in the rate law.
  • Molecularity: Number of reacting species in an elementary reaction.
  • Rate Constant: Constant that relates rate of reaction with concentration.
  • Half-Life: Time required for reactant concentration to become half.
  • Activation Energy: Minimum energy required for reaction to occur.
  • Catalyst: Substance that increases reaction rate without being consumed.

Main Concepts

Rate of Reaction

Rate of reaction represents how quickly reactants are converted into products.

Rate = Change in concentration / Time

Factors Affecting Rate

  • Concentration: Higher concentration increases collision frequency.
  • Temperature: Higher temperature increases kinetic energy of molecules.
  • Catalyst: Provides an alternate pathway with lower activation energy.
  • Surface Area: Greater surface area increases reaction speed.

Order of Reaction

Order of reaction indicates how reaction rate depends on concentration of reactants.

  • Zero Order
  • First Order
  • Second Order

Molecularity

Molecularity represents the number of molecules involved in an elementary reaction step.

Half-Life

Half-life is the time required for half of the reactant to react.

Collision Theory

Reactions occur when molecules collide with sufficient energy and proper orientation.

Activation Energy

Activation energy is the minimum energy required for a reaction to occur.

Arrhenius Equation

The Arrhenius equation explains how temperature affects reaction rate.

Important Formulas

  • Rate: Rate = Δ[Concentration] / Δt
  • Rate Law: Rate = k[A]x[B]y
  • Order of Reaction: n = x + y
  • Zero Order: [A] = [A]₀ − kt
  • First Order: ln[A] = ln[A]₀ − kt
  • Half-life: t½ = 0.693 / k
  • Arrhenius Equation: k = Ae-Ea/RT

Important Points for Exams

  • Order of reaction is determined experimentally.
  • Molecularity applies only to elementary reactions.
  • Half-life of first-order reaction is independent of concentration.
  • Rate constant increases with temperature.
  • Catalysts lower activation energy.

Common Mistakes Students Make

  • Confusing order of reaction with molecularity.
  • Using incorrect units for rate constant.
  • Applying wrong rate equations.
  • Ignoring logarithmic form in first-order equations.
  • Assuming catalysts change equilibrium.

Quick Revision Summary

  • Rate depends on concentration, temperature, catalyst and surface area.
  • Rate law shows relation between rate and concentration.
  • Order of reaction is determined experimentally.
  • Activation energy determines reaction speed.
  • Arrhenius equation explains temperature effect.
