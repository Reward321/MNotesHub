Introduction

This chapter studies solutions, which are homogeneous mixtures of two or more substances. In everyday life, many mixtures such as salt water, sugar solution, air, and alloys are examples of solutions.

Understanding solutions is important in chemistry because the behavior of substances in solution helps explain many chemical and biological processes. The chapter focuses on how solutions are formed, how their concentrations are measured, and how their physical properties change when solutes are added.

A major part of the chapter deals with colligative properties, which depend only on the number of solute particles present in the solution and not on their nature. These properties are very important in determining molecular masses of substances and understanding phenomena such as boiling point elevation and freezing point depression.

Key Definitions

  • Solution: A homogeneous mixture of two or more chemically non-reacting substances.
  • Solvent: The component present in the largest amount in a solution that dissolves the solute.
  • Solute: The component present in smaller quantity which gets dissolved in the solvent.
  • Concentration of Solution: The amount of solute present in a given quantity of solvent or solution.
  • Solubility: The maximum amount of solute that can dissolve in a given amount of solvent at a specific temperature.
  • Raoult's Law: The partial vapour pressure of each component of an ideal solution is proportional to its mole fraction.
  • Colligative Properties: Properties of dilute solutions that depend only on the number of solute particles present in the solution.
  • Relative Lowering of Vapour Pressure: The decrease in vapour pressure of a solvent when a non-volatile solute is added.
  • Elevation of Boiling Point: Increase in boiling point of a solvent due to addition of a non-volatile solute.
  • Depression of Freezing Point: Decrease in freezing point of a solvent due to addition of a solute.
  • Osmosis: The flow of solvent molecules through a semipermeable membrane from a dilute solution to a concentrated solution.
  • Osmotic Pressure: The pressure required to stop the process of osmosis.
  • Van't Hoff Factor (i): A factor that accounts for association or dissociation of solute particles in solution.

Main Concepts

Types of Solutions

Solutions can be classified depending on the physical states of solute and solvent. Since each can exist in solid, liquid, or gaseous form, many types of solutions are possible.

  • Gas in Gas: Example: Air (oxygen dissolved in nitrogen).
  • Gas in Liquid: Example: Oxygen dissolved in water.
  • Liquid in Liquid: Example: Alcohol dissolved in water.
  • Solid in Liquid: Example: Salt dissolved in water.
  • Solid in Solid: Example: Alloys such as brass.

The most common solutions studied in this chapter are solid dissolved in liquid solutions.

Concentration of Solutions

Concentration expresses how much solute is present in a solution. Various methods are used to express concentration.

  • Mass percentage
  • Volume percentage
  • Mass by volume percentage
  • Mole fraction
  • Molarity
  • Molality

Molality is often preferred in colligative property calculations because it does not depend on temperature.

Solubility of Gases in Liquids

The solubility of gases in liquids depends mainly on:

  • Nature of gas and solvent
  • Temperature
  • Pressure

According to Henry's Law, the solubility of a gas in a liquid is directly proportional to the partial pressure of the gas above the liquid.

Solid Solutions

Solid solutions are homogeneous mixtures where the solvent is solid. These are commonly found in alloys.

  • Brass (Copper + Zinc)
  • Bronze (Copper + Tin)

Raoult's Law

Raoult's law describes the relationship between vapour pressure and mole fraction in an ideal solution.

Ideal solutions obey Raoult's law over the entire concentration range.

Colligative Properties

  • Relative lowering of vapour pressure
  • Elevation of boiling point
  • Depression of freezing point
  • Osmotic pressure

Determination of Molecular Mass

Colligative properties can be used to determine the molecular mass of unknown substances because these properties depend on the number of solute particles.

Abnormal Molecular Mass

Sometimes observed molecular mass differs from calculated value due to association or dissociation of solute molecules.

Van't Hoff Factor

Van't Hoff factor corrects colligative property equations when solute particles associate or dissociate in solution.

Important Formulas

Mole Fraction:

XA = nA / (nA + nB)

  • nA = moles of component A
  • nB = moles of component B

Raoult's Law:

PA = XA × PA0

  • PA = vapour pressure in solution
  • P°A = vapour pressure of pure component

Elevation of Boiling Point:

ΔTb = Kb × m

Depression of Freezing Point:

ΔTf = Kf × m

Osmotic Pressure:

π = CRT

  • C = molar concentration
  • R = gas constant
  • T = temperature (Kelvin)

Important Points for Exams

  • Colligative properties depend only on number of solute particles.
  • Molality is temperature independent.
  • Raoult's law is valid only for ideal solutions.
  • Elevation of boiling point and depression of freezing point are proportional to molality.
  • Osmotic pressure is widely used for determining molecular masses of macromolecules.

Common Mistakes Students Make

  • Confusing molarity and molality.
  • Forgetting temperature must be in Kelvin in osmotic pressure equation.
  • Incorrect mole fraction calculations.
  • Ignoring Van't Hoff factor in abnormal molecular mass problems.

Quick Revision Summary

  • Solutions are homogeneous mixtures.
  • Solute dissolves in solvent.
  • Raoult's law relates vapour pressure and mole fraction.
  • Colligative properties depend on number of solute particles.
  • Main colligative properties: boiling point elevation, freezing point depression, osmotic pressure.
  • Van't Hoff factor corrects abnormal molecular mass.
