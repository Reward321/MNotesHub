📘 Chapter 6: Haloalkanes and Haloarenes

1. Introduction

This chapter deals with organic compounds containing halogen atoms (F, Cl, Br, I) bonded to carbon.

  • Haloalkanes (Alkyl halides): Halogen attached to sp³ carbon
  • Haloarenes (Aryl halides): Halogen attached to aromatic ring

Importance:

  • Used in pharmaceuticals, solvents, refrigerants, pesticides
  • Important for understanding SN1, SN2, E1, E2 mechanisms
  • High weightage in CBSE exams

2. Classification of Haloalkanes

  • Primary (1°): R–CH₂–X
  • Secondary (2°): R₂CH–X
  • Tertiary (3°): R₃C–X

3. Nature of C–X Bond

  • C–X bond is polar
  • Carbon = δ⁺, Halogen = δ⁻
  • Carbon becomes susceptible to nucleophilic attack
  • Bond strength: C–F > C–Cl > C–Br > C–I

Leaving group ability: I⁻ > Br⁻ > Cl⁻ > F⁻

4. Key Definitions

  • Nucleophile: Electron-rich species (OH⁻, CN⁻)
  • Electrophile: Electron-deficient species (H⁺)
  • SN1 Reaction: Two-step substitution via carbocation
  • SN2 Reaction: One-step substitution
  • Optical Isomerism: Rotation of plane-polarized light

5. Preparation

A. Haloalkanes

1. From Alcohols

R–OH + HX → R–X + H₂O

Reagents: HCl/ZnCl₂, HBr, HI

2. From Alkanes

RH + Cl₂ → RCl + HCl (UV light)

3. From Alkenes

RCH=CH₂ + HBr → RCHBr–CH₃

Markovnikov rule; Anti-Markovnikov in peroxide

4. Finkelstein Reaction

R–Cl + NaI → R–I + NaCl

5. Swarts Reaction

R–Cl + AgF → R–F

B. Haloarenes

1. From Benzene

C₆H₆ + Cl₂ → C₆H₅Cl + HCl (FeCl₃)

2. Sandmeyer Reaction

C₆H₅N₂⁺Cl⁻ + CuCl → C₆H₅Cl + N₂

6. Reactions

A. Haloalkanes

1. Nucleophilic Substitution

SN1:

  • Two-step, carbocation intermediate
  • Reactivity: 3° > 2° > 1°
  • Polar protic solvent
  • Shows racemization

SN2:

  • One-step, backside attack
  • Reactivity: 1° > 2° > 3°
  • Polar aprotic solvent
  • Shows inversion (Walden inversion)

2. Elimination (E2)

CH₃CH₂Br + alc. KOH → CH₂=CH₂ + KBr + H₂O

3. Wurtz Reaction

2R–X + 2Na → R–R + 2NaX

4. Reduction

R–X + H₂ → R–H + HX (Pd/Ni)

B. Haloarenes

1. Nucleophilic Substitution

Requires high temperature and pressure

2. Electrophilic Substitution

Halogens are deactivating but ortho-para directing

3. Wurtz-Fittig Reaction

Ar–X + R–X + Na → Ar–R

4. Fittig Reaction

2Ar–X + 2Na → Ar–Ar

13. Additional Important Reactions

Friedel–Crafts Reaction

C₆H₆ + R–Cl → C₆H₅–R + HCl (AlCl₃)

C₆H₆ + RCOCl → C₆H₅COR + HCl (AlCl₃)

Haloarenes do not undergo this reaction easily due to deactivation.

Grignard Reagent

R–X + Mg → R–MgX (dry ether)

  • Highly reactive nucleophile
  • Used to form alcohols and acids

7. Why Haloarenes are Less Reactive

  • Resonance stabilization
  • Partial double bond character
  • sp² hybridised carbon

8. Important Concepts

  • Carbocation stability: 3° > 2° > 1°
  • Protic solvent → SN1
  • Aprotic solvent → SN2
  • Halogens show +R and –I effect

9. Polyhalogen Compounds

Chloroform (CHCl₃)

  • Used as solvent

Carbon Tetrachloride (CCl₄)

  • Used in fire extinguishers

Iodoform (CHI₃)

  • Yellow precipitate

Iodoform Test: Compounds with –COCH₃ group give yellow ppt

10. Common Mistakes

  • Confusing SN1 and SN2
  • Ignoring steric hindrance
  • Wrong application of Markovnikov rule
  • Assuming haloarenes react easily

11. Quick Revision

  • Haloalkanes: sp³ carbon
  • Haloarenes: aromatic ring
  • SN1 → carbocation
  • SN2 → inversion
  • Alcoholic KOH → elimination
  • Aqueous KOH → substitution

12. MCQs

Ans: Inversion

Ans: Resonance
