Introduction

A solution is a homogeneous mixture of two or more substances. This chapter deals with types of solutions, concentration of solutions, solubility of gases, Raoult's law, and colligative properties. These properties depend only on the number of solute particles and are widely used to determine molecular mass.

Key Definitions

  • Solution: Homogeneous mixture of two or more substances.
  • Solvent: Component present in larger amount.
  • Solute: Component present in smaller amount.
  • Molarity (M): Moles of solute per litre of solution (mol L⁻¹).
  • Molality (m): Moles of solute per kilogram of solvent (mol kg⁻¹).
  • Mole Fraction (X): Ratio of moles of a component to total moles.
  • Henry's Law: Solubility of a gas in a liquid is directly proportional to its partial pressure.
  • Ideal Solution: Obeys Raoult's law at all concentrations and has ΔHmix = 0 and ΔVmix = 0.
  • Non-Ideal Solution: Deviates from Raoult's law.
  • Azeotrope: Mixture that boils at constant temperature and composition.
  • Colligative Properties: Properties that depend only on the number of solute particles.
  • Osmotic Pressure: Pressure required to stop osmosis.
  • Van't Hoff Factor (i): Ratio of actual number of particles to expected number.

Main Concepts

Types of Solutions

  • Gas in Gas → Air
  • Gas in Liquid → CO₂ in water
  • Liquid in Liquid → Ethanol in water
  • Solid in Liquid → NaCl in water
  • Solid in Solid → Alloys

Concentration of Solutions

  • Mass % = (mass of solute / mass of solution) × 100
  • Volume % = (volume of solute / volume of solution) × 100
  • ppm = (parts of solute / total parts) × 10⁶
  • Molarity (temperature dependent)
  • Molality (temperature independent)
  • Mole fraction (dimensionless)

Solubility of Gases in Liquids (Henry's Law)

p = KH × x

  • p = partial pressure of gas
  • x = mole fraction of gas
  • KH = Henry's constant (depends on gas and temperature)
  • Higher KH → lower solubility

Solid Solutions

  • Substitutional solid solutions → atoms replace each other
  • Interstitial solid solutions → small atoms occupy spaces

Raoult's Law for Binary Solutions

P₁ = X₁P₁° and P₂ = X₂P₂°

Total vapour pressure:

Ptotal = X₁P₁° + X₂P₂°

Non-Ideal Solutions

  • Positive deviation: weaker intermolecular forces → higher vapour pressure
  • Negative deviation: stronger intermolecular forces → lower vapour pressure

Azeotropes

  • Minimum boiling azeotrope → positive deviation
  • Maximum boiling azeotrope → negative deviation

Colligative Properties

Relative Lowering of Vapour Pressure

(P° − P)/P° = n₂ / n₁ (for dilute solutions)

Adding a non-volatile solute reduces vapour pressure because fewer solvent molecules escape into vapour phase.

Elevation of Boiling Point

Boiling point increases because vapour pressure decreases.

ΔTb = iKb m

Depression of Freezing Point

Freezing point decreases because solute particles disrupt formation of solid structure.

ΔTf = iKf m

Osmosis and Osmotic Pressure

Osmosis is the flow of solvent from dilute solution to concentrated solution through a semipermeable membrane.

π = iCRT

Determination of Molecular Mass

Molar mass of solute can be calculated using colligative property relations:

M₂ = (w₂ × 1000 × Kf) / (ΔTf × w₁)

Abnormal Molecular Mass

  • Dissociation increases number of particles → molar mass decreases
  • Association decreases number of particles → molar mass increases

Van't Hoff Factor

  • i = observed / calculated
  • Dissociation: i = 1 + (n − 1)α
  • Association: i = 1 − (n − 1)α

Important Formulas

M = n/V (mol L⁻¹)

m = n/W (mol kg⁻¹)

P = XP°

(P° − P)/P° = n₂/n₁

ΔTb = iKb m (K)

ΔTf = iKf m (K)

π = iCRT (Pa)

Important Points for Exams

  • Molality is preferred in colligative properties.
  • Ideal solutions: ΔHmix = 0, ΔVmix = 0.
  • Positive deviation → weaker interactions.
  • Negative deviation → stronger interactions.
  • Osmotic pressure is most accurate for molar mass determination.
  • Electrolytes show abnormal molecular mass.

Common Mistakes Students Make

  • Confusing molarity and molality.
  • Ignoring Van't Hoff factor.
  • Wrong unit conversions (g ↔ kg).
  • Incorrect formula substitution.
  • Forgetting dilute solution assumptions.

Quick Revision Summary

  • Solutions are homogeneous mixtures.
  • Raoult's law explains vapour pressure behavior.
  • Colligative properties depend on number of particles.
  • Boiling point increases, freezing point decreases.
  • Van't Hoff factor corrects abnormal behavior.

1. Which unit is temperature independent?

  • A. Molarity
  • B. Molality
  • C. Volume %
  • D. Density

Answer: B

2. Henry's law relates solubility with:

  • A. Temperature
  • B. Pressure
  • C. Volume
  • D. Density

Answer: B

3. Positive deviation occurs when:

  • A. Interactions increase
  • B. Interactions decrease
  • C. Pressure decreases
  • D. Temperature decreases

Answer: B

4. Colligative properties depend on:

  • A. Nature of solute
  • B. Number of particles
  • C. Temperature
  • D. Pressure

Answer: B

5. Azeotropes have:

  • A. Constant composition
  • B. Variable composition
  • C. No boiling point
  • D. Infinite pressure

Answer: A

6. Van't Hoff factor greater than 1 indicates:

  • A. Association
  • B. Dissociation
  • C. No reaction
  • D. Precipitation

Answer: B
