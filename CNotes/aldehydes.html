1. Introduction

This chapter deals with carbonyl compounds and carboxylic acids. Aldehydes (–CHO), Ketones (>C=O), and Carboxylic acids (–COOH) are important organic compounds used in industry, medicine, and daily life.

2. Key Definitions

  • Carbonyl Group: C=O functional group
  • Aldehyde: Compound containing –CHO
  • Ketone: Compound containing >C=O
  • Carboxylic Acid: Compound containing –COOH
  • Nucleophilic Addition: Addition of nucleophile to carbonyl carbon
  • Alpha Hydrogen: Hydrogen attached to carbon adjacent to carbonyl

3. Preparation

From Alcohols

Primary alcohol → Aldehyde

R–CH2OH + [O] → R–CHO + H2O (PCC)

Secondary alcohol → Ketone

R2CHOH + [O] → R2CO + H2O

Rosenmund Reduction

R–COCl + H2 → R–CHO (Pd/BaSO4)

Stephen Reaction

R–CN → R–CHO (SnCl2/HCl)

Ozonolysis

Alkene + O3 → Aldehyde/Ketone

Etard Reaction

C6H5–CH3 + CrO2Cl2 → C6H5–CHO

Used to convert toluene into benzaldehyde

Gattermann-Koch Reaction

Benzene + CO + HCl → Benzaldehyde (AlCl3/CuCl)

Preparation of Carboxylic Acids

  • Oxidation of aldehydes: R–CHO → R–COOH
  • Oxidation of alcohols: R–CH2OH → R–COOH
  • From nitriles: R–CN → R–COOH
  • Grignard reagent: R–MgX + CO2 → R–COOH

4. Reactions

Nucleophilic Addition Mechanism

  • Nucleophile attacks electrophilic carbonyl carbon
  • Tetrahedral intermediate forms
  • Protonation gives final product

Important Addition Reactions

  • HCN addition: R–CHO + HCN → R–CH(OH)CN
  • NaHSO3 addition: forms addition compound
  • Grignard reaction: forms alcohol

Reduction

R–CHO → R–CH2OH (NaBH4 / LiAlH4)

Oxidation

Aldehydes oxidize to acids

Tollens' Test

R–CHO + Ag+ → Silver mirror

Fehling's Test

R–CHO + Cu2+ → Red precipitate (Cu2O)

Aldol Condensation

Occurs in presence of alpha hydrogen

Cannizzaro Reaction

Occurs when no alpha hydrogen is present

Clemmensen Reduction

R–CO–R → R–CH2–R (Zn/Hg + HCl)

Wolff-Kishner Reduction

R–CO–R → R–CH2–R (NH2NH2/KOH)

HVZ Reaction

R–CH2COOH → R–CHXCOOH (Br2/P)

Carboxylic Acid Reactions

  • Esterification: R–COOH + ROH → Ester
  • Reaction with metals → Salt + H2
  • Decarboxylation → Alkane + CO2
  • Reduction → Alcohol

5. Important Exam Points

  • Aldehydes are more reactive than ketones
  • Reason: Less steric hindrance and +I effect
  • Order: Formaldehyde > Aldehyde > Ketone
  • Carboxylic acids are acidic due to resonance
  • Electron-withdrawing groups increase acidity
  • Tollens gives silver mirror
  • Fehling gives red ppt

6. Common Mistakes

  • Confusing aldehydes and ketones in oxidation
  • Forgetting alpha hydrogen condition
  • Incorrect reaction conditions
  • Ignoring resonance in acidity

7. Quick Revision

  • Functional groups: –CHO, >C=O, –COOH
  • Main reactions: Aldol, Cannizzaro, Esterification
  • Tests: Tollens, Fehling
  • Name reactions: Etard, HVZ, Clemmensen

8. Concept-Based MCQs

  1. Why are aldehydes more reactive than ketones?
    Answer: Less steric hindrance
  2. Which gives Cannizzaro reaction?
    Answer: HCHO
  3. Product of esterification?
    Answer: Ester
  4. Tollens test detects?
    Answer: Aldehyde
  5. Fehling test gives?
    Answer: Red precipitate
  6. Aldol requires?
    Answer: Alpha hydrogen
  7. Reduction of aldehyde gives?
    Answer: Alcohol
  8. Decarboxylation removes?
    Answer: CO2
