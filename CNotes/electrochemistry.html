Introduction

Electrochemistry studies the relationship between chemical reactions and electrical energy. It explains how chemical reactions generate electricity (batteries) and how electricity can drive chemical changes (electrolysis). This chapter is essential for understanding batteries, corrosion, and industrial electrolysis.

Key Definitions

  • Redox Reaction: Reaction involving oxidation and reduction.
  • Oxidation: Loss of electrons.
  • Reduction: Gain of electrons.
  • Anode: Electrode where oxidation occurs.
  • Cathode: Electrode where reduction occurs.
  • Electrolyte: Substance that conducts electricity in solution.
  • EMF: Potential difference between electrodes.
  • Standard Electrode Potential: Potential under standard conditions.
  • Conductance: Ability to conduct electricity.
  • Molar Conductivity: Conductivity of one mole of electrolyte.

Main Concepts

Redox Reactions

Electrochemical reactions involve electron transfer. Oxidation occurs at anode and reduction at cathode.

Electrochemical Cells

  • Galvanic Cell: Converts chemical energy to electrical energy.
  • Electrolytic Cell: Uses electrical energy to drive reactions.

EMF of a Cell

EMF is the driving force of electron flow between electrodes.

Standard Electrode Potential

Measured relative to the standard hydrogen electrode (SHE).

Nernst Equation

Used to calculate electrode potential under non-standard conditions.

Gibbs Energy Relation

Determines spontaneity of electrochemical reactions.

Conductance

  • Depends on ions present
  • Increases with dilution (weak electrolytes)

Kohlrausch's Law

Molar conductivity at infinite dilution is sum of ionic conductivities.

Electrolysis

Chemical decomposition by electric current.

Types of Cells

  • Dry Cell
  • Lead Accumulator
  • Fuel Cells

Corrosion

Slow oxidation of metals (e.g., rusting).

Important Formulas

EMF of Cell: E = Ecathode − Eanode

Nernst Equation:

E = E° − (0.0591/n) log Q

Gibbs Energy: ΔG = −nFE

Conductivity: κ = G × cell constant

Molar Conductivity: Λm = κ × (1000 / C)

Faraday Law: m = ZIt

Important Points for Exams

  • Anode is negative in galvanic cell.
  • Cathode is positive in galvanic cell.
  • Higher reduction potential = stronger oxidizing agent.
  • EMF > 0 → spontaneous reaction.
  • Corrosion is electrochemical oxidation.
  • Lead accumulator is rechargeable.

Common Mistakes Students Make

  • Confusing anode and cathode.
  • Wrong sign in EMF calculation.
  • Incorrect use of Nernst equation.
  • Ignoring units in conductivity.
  • Forgetting salt bridge function.

Quick Revision Summary

  • Electrochemistry links electricity and reactions.
  • Galvanic cell produces electricity.
  • Nernst equation for non-standard conditions.
  • Conductivity depends on ions.
  • Electrolysis follows Faraday's laws.
  • Corrosion is oxidation.

1. Oxidation occurs at:

  • A. Cathode
  • B. Anode
  • C. Electrolyte
  • D. Salt bridge

Answer: B

2. EMF becomes zero when:

  • A. Reaction completes
  • B. Starts
  • C. Temperature rises
  • D. Pressure increases

Answer: A

3. Unit of molar conductivity:

  • A. S cm² mol⁻¹
  • B. Volt
  • C. Ohm
  • D. Ampere

Answer: A

4. Nernst equation applies to:

  • A. Standard conditions
  • B. Non-standard conditions
  • C. Only solids
  • D. Only gases

Answer: B

5. Corrosion is:

  • A. Reduction
  • B. Oxidation
  • C. Neutralization
  • D. Sublimation

Answer: B
