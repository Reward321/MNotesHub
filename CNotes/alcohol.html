1. Introduction

This chapter deals with organic compounds containing oxygen in the form of –OH (hydroxyl) and –O– (ether linkage).

  • Alcohols (R–OH): –OH attached to aliphatic carbon
  • Phenols (Ar–OH): –OH attached directly to benzene ring
  • Ethers (R–O–R'): Oxygen between two alkyl/aryl groups

Importance:

  • Used in medicines, fuels, antiseptics, solvents
  • Important in industrial chemistry and daily life
  • Frequently asked in CBSE exams

2. Key Definitions

  • Alcohol: –OH bonded to sp³ carbon
  • Phenol: –OH attached to benzene ring
  • Ether: Oxygen bonded to two alkyl/aryl groups
  • Primary Alcohol (1°): Carbon attached to one carbon
  • Secondary Alcohol (2°): Carbon attached to two carbons
  • Tertiary Alcohol (3°): Carbon attached to three carbons
  • Hydrogen Bonding: Attraction between –OH groups → high boiling point
  • Electrophilic Substitution: Replacement of H in aromatic ring
  • Lucas Test: Distinguishes 1°, 2°, 3° alcohols based on turbidity

3. Preparation

A. Preparation of Alcohols

1. From Alkenes (Hydration)

CH₂=CH₂ + H₂O → CH₃CH₂OH (H⁺, dilute H₂SO₄)

Follows Markovnikov's rule: –OH attaches to more substituted carbon

2. From Alkyl Halides

R–X + KOH (aq) → R–OH + KX

Nucleophilic substitution:

SN1: Tertiary halides (via carbocation)

SN2: Primary halides (one-step reaction)

3. Reduction of Carbonyl Compounds

RCHO → RCH₂OH (Primary alcohol)

RCOR' → RCHOHR' (Secondary alcohol)

Reagents: NaBH₄ / LiAlH₄

4. Grignard Reagent

RMgX + HCHO → RCH₂OH (followed by hydrolysis)

B. Preparation of Phenols

1. Dow's Process

C₆H₅Cl + NaOH → C₆H₅ONa (623K, 300 atm)

C₆H₅ONa + HCl → C₆H₅OH

2. From Benzene Sulphonic Acid

C₆H₅SO₃H + NaOH → C₆H₅ONa + Na₂SO₃

C₆H₅ONa + HCl → C₆H₅OH

C. Preparation of Ethers

1. Williamson Ether Synthesis

R–O⁻Na⁺ + R'X → R–O–R' + NaX

Mechanism: SN2 (best with primary alkyl halides)

2. Dehydration of Alcohols

2R–OH → R–O–R + H₂O (conc. H₂SO₄, 413K)

4. Reactions

A. Reactions of Alcohols

1. Reaction with Sodium

2R–OH + 2Na → 2R–ONa + H₂

2. Dehydration (Alkene Formation)

R–CH₂CH₂OH → R–CH=CH₂ + H₂O (conc. H₂SO₄, 443K)

3. Oxidation

Primary: RCH₂OH → RCHO → RCOOH

Secondary: RCHOHR' → RCOR'

Tertiary: No oxidation under normal conditions

B. Reactions of Phenols

1. Acidic Nature

C₆H₅OH + NaOH → C₆H₅ONa + H₂O

Reason: Phenoxide ion is stabilized by resonance

2. Nitration

C₆H₅OH + HNO₃ → o-nitrophenol + p-nitrophenol

3. Bromination

C₆H₅OH + 3Br₂ → 2,4,6-tribromophenol (white ppt)

4. Kolbe's Reaction (Kolbe–Schmitt Reaction)

C₆H₅ONa + CO₂ → o-hydroxybenzoic acid (salicylic acid)

Conditions: CO₂ gas, 373–403 K, followed by acidification

Explanation: Introduction of –COOH group at ortho position

5. Reimer–Tiemann Reaction

C₆H₅OH + CHCl₃ + NaOH → o-hydroxybenzaldehyde

Conditions: CHCl₃, NaOH, heat

Explanation: Introduction of –CHO group at ortho position

C. Reactions of Ethers

1. Cleavage with HI

R–O–R' + HI (conc., heat) → R–I + R'–OH

5. Important Points for Exams

  • Phenol is more acidic than alcohol (resonance stabilization)
  • Alcohols have high boiling points due to hydrogen bonding
  • Williamson synthesis works best with primary halides
  • 413K → Ether formation
  • 443K → Alkene formation
  • Phenol gives white ppt with bromine water
  • Lucas test distinguishes alcohol types
  • Oxidation: 1° → Acid, 2° → Ketone, 3° → No oxidation

6. Common Mistakes

  • Confusing phenol and alcohol acidity
  • Wrong application of Markovnikov rule
  • Using Williamson synthesis with tertiary halides
  • Forgetting reaction conditions
  • Writing incorrect oxidation products
  • Mixing up SN1 and SN2

7. Quick Revision

  • Alcohol: R–OH
  • Phenol: Ar–OH
  • Ether: R–O–R
  • Phenol most acidic
  • Ether least reactive

8. MCQs

  1. Most acidic compound?
    Answer: Phenol
  2. Williamson synthesis mechanism?
    Answer: SN2
  3. Secondary alcohol oxidation gives?
    Answer: Ketone
  4. Phenol + Br₂ gives?
    Answer: 2,4,6-tribromophenol
  5. Ether + HI gives?
    Answer: Alkyl iodide + alcohol
  6. Lucas test is used for?
    Answer: Alcohol classification
