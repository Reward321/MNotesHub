Chapter 5: Coordination Compounds

1. Introduction

Coordination compounds are compounds in which a central metal atom or ion is bonded to surrounding ions or molecules called ligands through coordinate bonds.

  • Biological systems (haemoglobin, chlorophyll)
  • Metallurgy (extraction of metals)
  • Medicine (chelation therapy)
  • Analytical chemistry (detection of ions)

2. Key Definitions

  • Coordination Compound: Compound with metal + ligands forming a complex entity
  • Central Metal Atom/Ion: Metal to which ligands are attached
  • Ligand: Species that donates lone pair (Lewis base)
  • Coordination Number (CN): Number of donor atoms attached to metal
  • Coordination Sphere: Part inside square brackets
  • Oxidation State: Charge on metal after ligand bonding
  • Double Salt: Dissociates completely in water (e.g., Mohr's salt)
  • Complex Compound: Does not fully dissociate (e.g., [Fe(CN)6]4−)
  • Chelate: Ring structure formed by polydentate ligand
  • Ambidentate Ligand: Can bind through different atoms (NO2−, SCN−)

3. Main Concepts

3.1 Difference: Double Salt vs Coordination Compound

Property Double Salt Coordination Compound
Dissociation Complete Partial
Identity in solution Lost Retained

3.2 Werner's Theory

  • Primary valency = oxidation state (ionisable)
  • Secondary valency = coordination number (non-ionisable)
  • Secondary valencies are directional and determine geometry
  • Ligands satisfy secondary valencies

3.3 Types of Ligands

  • Monodentate: NH3, Cl−
  • Bidentate: Ethylenediamine
  • Polydentate: EDTA
  • Ambidentate: NO2−

3.4 IUPAC Nomenclature

  1. Name ligands first in alphabetical order (ignore prefixes)
  2. Anionic ligands end with "-o" (chloro, cyano, hydroxo)
  3. Neutral ligands: NH3 (ammine), H2O (aqua), CO (carbonyl)
  4. Use prefixes: di, tri, tetra; bis, tris for complex ligands
  5. Write oxidation state in Roman numerals
  6. Anionic complex → metal ends with "-ate" (Fe → ferrate)

Example: [Co(NH3)6]Cl3 → Hexaamminecobalt(III) chloride

3.5 Isomerism

(A) Structural Isomerism

  • Ionisation: [Co(NH3)5Cl]SO4 and [Co(NH3)5SO4]Cl
  • Hydrate: [Cr(H2O)6]Cl3 and [Cr(H2O)5Cl]Cl2·H2O
  • Linkage: NO2− binds via N or O

(B) Stereoisomerism

  • Geometrical: cis/trans (square planar & octahedral)
  • Tetrahedral complexes do not show geometrical isomerism
  • Optical: Non-superimposable mirror images

3.6 Valence Bond Theory (VBT)

Hybridisation Geometry
sp3 Tetrahedral
dsp2 Square planar
d2sp3 Octahedral (inner orbital)
sp3d2 Octahedral (outer orbital)
  • Strong field ligands → pairing → inner orbital complex
  • Weak field ligands → no pairing → outer orbital complex

3.7 Crystal Field Theory (CFT)

  • Ligands treated as point charges
  • d-orbitals split due to repulsion
  • Octahedral: t2g (lower), eg (higher)
  • eg orbitals have higher energy due to direct interaction with ligands
  • Tetrahedral: reverse splitting, smaller splitting energy

Conditions:

  • Δ0 > P → Low spin
  • Δ0 < P → High spin

3.8 Colour in Coordination Compounds

  • Due to d–d transition
  • Electron absorbs light and jumps to higher level
  • Observed colour is complementary to absorbed colour
  • d0 and d10 → colourless

3.9 Magnetic Properties

  • Paramagnetic: unpaired electrons
  • Diamagnetic: no unpaired electrons

3.10 Magnetic Moment

μ = √(n(n+2)) BM

3.11 Effective Atomic Number (EAN)

EAN = Z − oxidation state + electrons donated

3.12 Stability of Coordination Compounds

  • Depends on charge, size of metal, and nature of ligand
  • Chelate effect increases stability

3.13 Metal Carbonyls

  • Example: Ni(CO)4
  • CO is a strong field ligand

3.14 Applications

  • Haemoglobin: transport of oxygen
  • Chlorophyll: photosynthesis
  • EDTA: chelation therapy
  • Cyanide process: extraction of gold
  • Analytical chemistry: detection of metal ions

4. Important Formulas

  • μ = √(n(n+2)) BM
  • EAN = Z − oxidation state + electrons donated

5. Important Points for Exams

  • Coordination number determines geometry
  • Strong ligands → low spin
  • Weak ligands → high spin
  • Tetrahedral → no geometrical isomerism
  • d0, d10 → colourless
  • Chelates are more stable
  • Nomenclature is highly scoring

6. Common Mistakes

  • Wrong oxidation state calculation
  • Ignoring alphabetical order
  • Confusing isomerism types
  • Not checking electron pairing in VBT
  • Mixing VBT and CFT concepts

7. Quick Revision Summary

  • Metal + ligands → coordination compound
  • Werner → structure
  • VBT → geometry
  • CFT → colour & magnetism
  • Isomerism → important
  • Chelate effect → stability
